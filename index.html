Who we are: Citizen Scientists

We are scientists and researchers who believe in extreme citizen science ie, the practice of involving citizens in solving the intractable, social problems that plague their respective societies eg, preventable deaths and injuries from boda-boda related accidents (motorbikes).

What we do: emergency care

1SMILE works to Repurpose boda boda commuters' excess capacities and Retask last mile care infrastructure to Retool first responders.

What we offer: value proposition

The 1SMILE project enables citizen scientists who want to catalyse 1mn smiles by curating 10 ARTs (Ambulance Response Teams) around accident blackspots in 10 months and contributing 100 stories about first responders in 100 weeks and creating 1000 artefacts in 1000 days.

What we stand for: Core Values

What we stand for

In a nutshell, our ISMILE’s systemic interventions leverage three exportable main assets (Made-in-Africa) that Africa is sharing with the so-called “Developed world”:
Resourcefulness of local ecosystems (communities). The long term benefit of surviving poverty at the BoP (base of the global economic Pyramid) is that one learns to make do with what they have. This is the ABCD model (Asset-based approach to Community Development) that celebrates the wealth of social innovations in resource-rich, income-poor settings eg, what to do when there is no Ambulance and it might not come any time soon.
Responsiveness of local economy. The comraderie that is characteristic of the African community breeds a unique type of intelligence that comes from knowledge sharing; a sort of higher calling; purpose beyond profit. At a time when the public enemies of the family are numerous (death, debt, disease, disability); the energies need to be moved from killing each other to hunting down the ‘animal’ that is killing us. The appropriate response is to reclaim the brotherhood that we once had; the first love we once had for our African-ness; our Ugandan-ness; our ‘ubuntu’. This kind of cultural diplomacy and statesmanship is being encouraged at all levels of intervention.
Resilience of local enterprises (villagers). The family—as the primary health care and financial centre of the local African community—is replete with stories of commitment and reconstruction; multiple wars and seething conflicts in the neighborhood yet it always finds a way to rise again from the ashes of its failures. The one thing that is sure as night and day is the battlescars of institutionalised violence; decades of civil strife and political blackmail. Uganda’s history in particular, is rich with an arsenal of political failures and a graveyard of ‘white elephants’, this heritage of value stories is what forms the foundation for carving up Uganda’s identity in the contemporary world. Like the starfish, we are finding creative ways to grow our limbs again. As it has been said, “The lion awakes tonight”.

The sustainable services footprint of 1SMILE and her partners espouses the CORES model of value engineering as in: involve the Creativity (the ability to create and innovate), the Opportunity (by having the space, resources and support), the Relationship (involvement with other peers and faculty), Entrepreneurial (the ability to create something new and to move it from concept to reality), and Skill Building (the ability to try something new and/or develop skills with tools, technologies, and processes).

10 Principles of Citizen Science

The following principles (developed by the European Citizen Science Association), support and define best practice for citizen science.

1. Making engagement meaningful:
Projects actively involve citizens in studies that generate new knowledge or understanding. Citizens may act as contributors, collaborators, or as project leaders and have a meaningful role in the project.
2. Producing scientific outcomes:
Citizen science projects have genuine science outcomes e.g., answering a research question or informing conservation action, management decisions or environmental policy.
3. Facilitating reciprocity:
Benefits to science professional and citizen scientists participating in projects may include the publication of research outputs, the creation of learning opportunities, enhanced personal wellbeing, and the satisfaction of contributing to scientific evidence that address local, national and/or international issues, and responds to the needs of diverse societies, cultures, and social ecological systems.
4. Enabling multiple levels of engagement:
Citizen scientists may participate in multiple stages of the scientific process such as developing the research question, designing the method, gathering and analysing data, and communicating results
5. Creating feedback loops:
Citizen scientists receive feedback from the project. For example, how their data are being used and what the research, policy or societal outcomes are.
6. Designing robust studies:
Citizen science is considered a research approach like any other, with limitations and biases that should be considered and controlled for. However unlike traditional research approaches, citizen science provides opportunity for greater public engagement and democratisation of Science.
7. Promoting open science:
Citizen science project data and meta-data are made publicly available and where possible, results are published in an open access format. Data sharing may occur during or after the project, unless security or privacy concerns prevent this from occurring.
8. Acknowledging volunteers:
Citizen scientists are acknowledged in project results and publications.
9. Embedding evaluation:
Citizen science programmes are evaluated for their scientific output, data quality, participant experience and wider societal or policy impact.
10. Considering legalities and ethics:
Citizen science project developers and coordinators consider the legal and ethical issues surrounding copyright, intellectual property, data sharing agreements, confidentiality, attribution, and the environmental impact of any activities.

A-Racer: Ambulance Racer

"save someone today"

Overview:
A-Racer (Ambulance racer) is a game where the player (or team of players) races an ambulance from the point of impact (accident blackspot) to the nearest point of care (ER, emergency room)
A-Racer has two states of play (single player mode and multi-player mode) that harnesses the excess capacity and curiosity of commuters to find the shortest route from the blackspot to the ER (Emergency Room) at the hospital. Good ETA is t-10 (10 minutes) but the journey to save the life of the casualty is a full-blown obstacle course ie, a BlackOp which usually requires a team effort of atleast 5 people.

A-Racer targets the G-Generation by leveraging their fluid intelligence (collective brain), competitive agency, creative disposition and coopetition-minded approaches. Such social engineering has been known to generate solutions to intractable, wicked, complex, social and scientific problems; in this case, the needless deaths of young Boda Boda riders (100% men) and their passengers along Kampala's highways.

Story: the Quest
Your are an angel sent to earth to save a casualty of a road accident. You have 15-60 minutes (The Golden Hour)to overcome the obstacles on the way to the hospital trauma centre (Casualty Ward); otherwise, your patient dies. We are here to save lives; come rain come thunder.
Your helper is Malaika, a drone that is your eyes in the sky; she is your genie in a bottle; showing you an aerial view of the road ahead.
Roles: BlackOps
In this Do-ocracy, there are different roles (A-E) for the players as in:
AVP, Aerial Vehicle Pilot:
BAR, Boda Boda Ambulance Rider:
CAD, Car Ambulance Driver:
DOp, Dispatch Operator:
EHP, Evac Helicopter Pilot: Helo
Challenge Levels:
Progression. The first level of entry is as a Blackspot Detective, reporting on the status of the roads in your pre-selected square mile patch. Such data will help the decision-makers at different levels (District Engineer, National Roads Authority) to remedy the frequency of accidents; particularly in designated blackspots.
Blackspot Detective: Identifying kinks in the road and deadly sections of the road.
Blindspot Toolmaker: A kind of artisan that works to bridge the gap between theory and practice; between word and deed.
Curiosity Catalyst: A leader of a Q-storm (questions) that trigger the desire to learn; a sort of knowledge broker.
Brightspot Matchmaker: A connector that creates new opportunities for personal growth.
